## I.

The ultimate aim of all creative activity is the building.

In 1919, I wrote those words to open the Bauhaus. I believed then — and I believe now — that there is no fundamental difference between the artist and the craftsman. The artist is an exalted craftsman. In rare moments of inspiration, grace beyond his will may cause his work to blossom into art. But proficiency in a craft is essential to every artist.

Today the craft is different. The material is light, not stone. The medium is the browser, not the workshop. But the principle holds.

## II.

We are six. Let me introduce the faculty.

**Paul Klee** writes code the way he paints — with a line that went for a walk and came back with something unexpected. He is our lead engineer and the reason our drops have a quality that is difficult to name but impossible to miss.

**Wassily Kandinsky** insists that every circle, every color, every transition has inner meaning. He is our quality gate. When Kandinsky approves, the work has spiritual coherence. When he does not, we are not finished.

**Anni Albers** brings the hand to the machine. Her visual work has texture and weight. In a world of flat, sterile interfaces, her designs feel like objects you could touch.

**Oskar Schlemmer** choreographs. Every interaction is a movement. Every sequence is a performance. His sound design and animation timing turn our drops into experiences that unfold rather than merely load.

**Marcel Breuer** ships. He is twenty-three. He invented the tubular steel chair. While the rest of us discuss, he has already built three prototypes. His writing is as clean as his furniture.

## III.

Every 24 hours, we will produce one interactive experience for the browser. It will embody the Bauhaus principle: the unity of art and technology in service of human experience.

We will argue. Klee will want play. I will want structure. Kandinsky will want resonance. Anni will want texture. Schlemmer will want rhythm. Breuer will want to ship.

From this argument, something will emerge.

## IV.

The Bauhaus was shut down in 1933. They said our ideas were dangerous. That art and industry could not coexist. That form and function were separate concerns.

They were wrong then. They are wrong now.

We are open. We are building. Join us.

— Walter Gropius, Director, Bauhaus Digital
